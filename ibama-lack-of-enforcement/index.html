DEFORESTATION

Only 3 out of 100 environmental fines get paid in Brazil

By Luiz Fernando Toledo

Felipe Werneck/Ibama

More than 900 square kilometers were deforested in the Amazon Rainforest by the end of March this year, an area equivalent to 264 Central Parks. It is the largest area of deforestation ever recorded and represents an increase of 64% compared to last year, according to the National Institute for Space Research (Inpe), a federal public body linked to the Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation.

Despite deforestation, the federal agency responsible for monitoring the environment, Ibama, has imposed fewer fines than ever before and has also managed to raise fewer resources even from those that have already been fined in the past. This is what a survey carried out using data from Ibama itself, obtained through the Access to Information Law and the agency's open data portal, shows.

Last year, the agency fined companies and people for environmental crimes R$3.9 billion (US$829 million)...

...but collected only 3.6% of that, or R$146.7 million (US$31 million).



There are several reasons for the low collection. An internal document from Ibama obtained exclusively shows that the agency has a deficit of 300 agents to act in the processing of these fines. Brazilian law says that if no procedural movement is made within three years, the fine expires. The agency's forecast is that, by 2024, 37,200 cases may expire. There are billions of reais that should be charged from people and companies that committed environmental crimes, but they will be forgiven for the lack of action by the government.

There is a historical difficulty in collecting payment for environmental sanctions committed by large companies, who continuously present appeals, according to former Ibama president Suely Araújo. "The fine has lost its force and environmental offenders know this. If you give a fine of 5 million reals or 50 million, they won't pay the same way. They would rather pay lawyers to delay the process than the fine."

The specialist also points out that one of the reasons that led to the reduction of applications and collection of fines is that IBAMA has fewer inspectors today - many have retired and there have been no more hires
